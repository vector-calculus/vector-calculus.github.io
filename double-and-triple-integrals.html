Double Integrals Over Rectangles

Consider a function $f(x)$ defined for $x \in [a,b].$ We divide the interval $[a,b]$ into $n$-subintervals $\left[x_i,x_{i+1}\right]$ of equal width $\Delta x = (b-a)/n$ and we choose sample points $x_i^*$ in these subintervals to form the Riemann sum \[ \sum_{i=0}^{n-1}f\left(x_i^*\right)\Delta x. \] Then we take the limit of such sums as $n\to \infty$ to obtain the definite integral of $f$ from $a$ to $b:$ \[ \displaystyle \int_a^b f(x)dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} f\left(x_i^*\right)\Delta x \] For the special case where $f(x)\geq 0,$ the Riemann sum can be interpreted as the sum of the areas of the approximation rectangles, and $\int_a^b f(x)dx$ represents the area under the curve $y=f(x).$

In a similar way we consider a function of two variables defined on a closed rectangle

\[ R = [a,b]\times [c,d]=\left\{(x,y)\in \R^2 ~|~ a\leq x\leq b,~c\leq y\leq d\right\} \]

We first suppose that $f(x,y)\geq 0.$ The graph of $f$ is a surface with equation $y = f(x,y).$ Let $V$ be region that lies above $R$ and under the graph of $f,$ that is,

\[ V=\left\{(x,y)\in \R^3 ~|~ 0\leq z\leq f(x,y),~(x,y)\in R\right\} \]

Our goal here is to find the volume of $V.$

Divide $R$ into subrectangles by dividing $[a,b]$ into $m$ subintervals $[x_{i-1},x_i]$, each of width $$\Delta x =\frac{b-a}{m}$$ and $[c,d]$ into $n$ subintervals $[y_{i-1},y_i]$ of equal width $$\Delta y = \frac{d-c}{n}.$$

Combining these gives a rectangular grid $R_{ij}$ with subrectangles each of area $\Delta A= \Delta x\Delta y.$ In each subrectangle take any point $P_{ij}$ with co-ordinates $\left(x_{ij}^*, y_{ij}^*\right).$

The volume of the box with base the rectangle $\Delta A$ and height the value of the function $f(x,y)$ at the point $P_{ij}$ (so the box touches the surface at a point directly above $P_{ij}$) is \[ V_{ij} = f\left(x_{ij}^*, y_{ij}^*\right)\Delta A \]

Then for all the subrectangles we have an approximation to the required volume $V$: \[ V\approx \sum_{i=1}^m\sum_{j=1}^n f\left(x_{ij}^*, y_{ij}^*\right) \Delta A, \] the double Riemann sum.

Let $\Delta x \to 0 $ and $\Delta y \to 0,$ i.e., $m\to \infty$ and $n\to \infty,$ then we define the volume to be \[ V= \lim_{m,n\to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^m\sum_{j=1}^n f\left(x_{ij}^*, y_{ij}^*\right) \Delta A, \] if the limits exist, and we write this as $$\ds \iint_Rf(x,y)~dA. $$

The double integral of $f$ over the rectangle $R$ is
\[ \iint_Rf(x,y)~dA = \lim_{m,n\to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^m\sum_{j=1}^n f\left(x_{ij}^*, y_{ij}^*\right) \Delta A \]
if the limit exists.

We call $f$ integrable if the limit exists. Note that every continuous function is integrable.


Iterated Integrals

It is often challenging to evaluate single integrals directly using the definition of an integral. However, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus offers a more straightforward approach. Evaluating double integrals from first principles is even more complex, but we can simplify the process by expressing a double integral as an iterated integral, allowing us to compute it by evaluating two single integrals sequentially.

We define $\displaystyle \int_c^df(x,y)~dy$ to mean that $x$ is fixed and $f(x,y)$ is integrated with respect to $y$ from $y=c$ to $y=d$. So \[ A(x) = \int_c^df(x,y)~dy \] is a function of $x$ only. If we now integrate $A(x)$ with respect to $x$ from $x=a$ to $x=b$ we have

\begin{eqnarray}\label{iter-int-01} \int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y)~dy ~dx &=& \int_a^b\left[ \int_c^d f(x,y)~dy\right]dx \end{eqnarray}

This is called the iterated integral.

Similarly, we have the iterated integral

\begin{eqnarray}\label{iter-int-02} \int_c^d \int_a^b f(x,y)~dx ~dy&=& \int_c^d\left[ \int_a^b f(x,y)~dx\right]dy \end{eqnarray}

In this case we first integrate with respect to $x$ (holding $y$ fixed) from $x=a$ to $x=b$ and then we integrate the resulting function of $y$ with respect to $y$ from $y=c$ to $y=d.$ Notice that in both equations (\ref{iter-int-01}) and (\ref{iter-int-02}) we work from the inside out.

Evaluate $\displaystyle {\small \int_0^2 \int_1^3 x^2y~dy~dx}.$

Integrating first with respect of $y$, and then with respect of $x,$ we obtain

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_0^2 \int_1^3 x^2y~dy~dx &= & \int_0^2 \left[ \int_1^3 x^2y~dy \right]dx \\ &=& \int_0^2 \left[ x^2 \frac{y^2}{2}\bigg|_{y=1}^{y=3} \right]dx\\ &=& \int_0^2 \left( x^2 ~\frac{3^2}{2} - x^2 ~\frac{1^2}{2} \right)dx \\ &=& \int_0^2 4x^2~dx \\ &=& \frac{4}{3}x^3\Bigg|_{x=0}^{x=2} \\ &=& \frac{4}{3} \left( 2^3 - 0^3 \right) \\ &=& \frac{32}{3}. \end{eqnarray*}

On the one other hand, we get

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_1^3 \int_0^2 x^2y~dx~dy &= & \int_1^3 \left[ \int_0^2 x^2y~dx \right]dy \\ &=& \int_1^3 \left[ y \frac{x^3}{3}\bigg|_{x=0}^{x=2} \right]dy \\ &=& \int_0^2 \left( y~ \frac{2^3}{3} - y ~\frac{0^3}{3} \right)dy \\ &=& \int_1^3 \frac{8}{3}y~dy \\ &=& \frac{8}{3} ~\frac{1}{2}~ y^2\Bigg|_{x=1}^{x=3} \\ &=& \frac{4}{3} \left(3^2 - 1^2 \right) \\ &=& \frac{32}{3}. \end{eqnarray*}

Fubini's Theorem

Observe that in Example 1, we arrived at the same result regardless of whether we integrated first with respect to $y$ or $x.$ More generally, the two iterated integrals in equations (\ref{iter-int-01}) and (\ref{iter-int-02}) are always equal, meaning the order of integration does not affect the outcome. The following theorem provides a practical approach for evaluating a double integral by expressing it as an iterated integral in either order.

The following theorem gives a practical method for evaluating a double integral by expressing it as an iterated integral (in either order).

If $f(x,y)$ is continuous on the rectangle $ R = [a,b]\times [c,d],$ then
\[ \iint_R f(x,y)~ dA = \int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y) ~dy~dx= \int_c^d \int_a^b f(x,y)~dx~dy. \]
First, let's prove \[ \iint_R f(x,y)~ dA = \int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y) ~dy~dx. \]

Let $c= y_0 \lt y_1 \lt \cdots \lt y_m =d$ be a partition of $[c,d]$ into $m$ equal parts. Define \[ F(x) = \int_c^d f(x,y) ~dy. \] Then \[ F(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{m-1}\int_{y_k}^{y_{k+1}}f(x,y)~dy. \] Using the integral version of the mean-value theorem 📃, This states that if $g(x)$ is continuous on $[a,b],$ then $\int_a^b g(x)~dx = g(c)(b-a)$ for some point $c\in[a,b].$ for each fixed $x$ and for each $k$ we have

\[ \int_{y_k}^{y_{k+1}}f(x,y)~dy = f\left(x, Y_k(x)\right)\left(y_{k+1}-y_k\right) \]

where the point $Y_k(x)$ belongs to $[y_k,y_{k+1}]$ and may depend on $x,$ $k,$ and $m.$

Thus we have that

\[ F(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{m-1} f\left(x, Y_k(x)\right)\left(y_{k+1}-y_k\right). \]

By the definition of the integral in one variable as a limit of Riemann sums,

\[ \int_a^b F(x)~dx = \int_a^b\left[ \int_c^d f(x,y)~dy\right]dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} F\left(p_j\right)\left(x_{j+1}-x_j\right), \]

where $a= x_0 \lt x_1 \lt \cdots \lt x_n =b$ is a partition of the interval $[a,b]$ into equal parts and $p_j$ is any point in $[x_j,x_{j+1}].$ By setting $c_{jk}=(p_j, Y_k(p_j))\in R_{jk},$ we have

\[ F(p_j) =\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f\left(c_{jk}\right)\left(y_{k+1}-y_k\right). \]

Therefore

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y)~dy~dx &=& \int_a^b F(x)~dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} F\left(p_j\right)\left(x_{j+1}-x_j\right) \\ &=& \lim_{m,n \to \infty} \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} \sum_{k=0}^{m-1} f\left(c_{jk}\right)\left(y_{k+1}-y_k\right)\left(x_{j+1}-x_j\right) \\ &=& \iint_R f(x,y)~dA. \end{eqnarray*}

Using a similar argument we can also prove that

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_d^c \int_a^b f(x,y)~dx~dy = \iint_R f(x,y)~dA. \end{eqnarray*}

Of course, Fubini's theorem can be generalized to the case where $f$ is not necessarily continuous.

Let's evalue the integral $\iint_R \left(x^2+y^2\right)dA,$ where $R = \left\{ (x,y)~|~0\leq x\leq 2, 0\leq y\leq 1 \right\}.$
\begin{eqnarray*} \iint_R \left(x^2+y^2\right)dA &=& \int_{x=0}^{x=2} \left[ \int_{y=0}^{y=1} \left(x^2+y^2\right)dy \right]dx \\ &=& \displaystyle \int_0^2 \left[ \left( x^2 y + \frac{y^3}{3} \right) \bigg|_{y=0}^{y=1} \right] dx \\ &=& \int_0^2 \left[ x^2 (1) + \frac{(1)^3}{3} - \left(x^2 (0) + x^2 \frac{(0)^3}{3}\right) \right]dx\\ &=& \int_0^2 \left( x^2+\frac{1}{3} \right) dx \\ &=& \left( \frac{x^3}{3}+\frac{1}{3}x \right)\Bigg|_{x=0}^{x=2} \\ &=& \frac{10}{3}. \end{eqnarray*}

On the other hand

\begin{eqnarray*} \iint_R \left(x^2+y^2\right)dA &=& \int_{y=0}^{y=1} \left[ \int_{x=0}^{x=2} \left(x^2+y^2\right)dx \right]dy \\ &=& \int_0^1 \left[ \left( \frac{x^3}{3} + y^2x \right) \bigg|_{x=0}^{x=2} \right] dy \\ &=& \int_0^1 \left[ \frac{(2)^3}{3} + y^2(2) - \left(\frac{(0)^3}{3} + y^2(0)\right) \right]dy \\ &=& \int_0^1 \left( \frac{8}{3} + 2y^2 \right) dy \\ &=& \left( \frac{8}{3}y + 2\frac{y^3}{3} \right)\Bigg|_{y=0}^{y=1} \\ &=& \frac{10}{3}. \end{eqnarray*}

In the special case where $f(x,y),$ can be factored as the product of a function of $x$ only and a function of $y$ only, the double integral of $f$ can be written in a particularly simple form. That is, suppose that $f(x,y) = g(x)h(y)$ and $R= [a,b]\times[c,d].$ Then Fubini's Theorem gives

\begin{eqnarray*} \iint_R f(x,y)~ dA &=& \int_c^d \int_a^b g(x)h(y)~dx~dy \\ &=& \int_c^d\left[\int_a^bg(x)h(y)~dx\right]dy. \end{eqnarray*}

In the inner integral, $y$ is a constant, so $h(y)$ is a constant and we can write

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_c^d\left[\int_a^bg(x)h(y)~dx\right]dy &= & \int_c^d\left[h(y) \left( \int_{a}^{b}g(x)~dx \right) \right]dy\\ &=& \int_a^b g(x)~dx \int_{c}^{d} h(y)~dy. \end{eqnarray*}

since $\int_a^b g(x)~dx$ is a constant. Hence, if $f$ can be written as the product of two integrals:

\begin{eqnarray*} \iint_R f(x,y)~ dA &=& \int_a^b g(x)~dx \int_{c}^{d} h(y)~dy. \end{eqnarray*}
Evaluate $\iint_R \sin x \cos y~dA$ where $ R = \left[ 0, \dfrac{\pi}{2} \right]\times \left[ 0, \dfrac{\pi}{2} \right].$

\begin{eqnarray*} \iint_R \sin x \cos y~dA &=& \left(\int_{x=0}^{x=\pi/2} \sin x ~dx\right) \left( \int_{y=0}^{y=\pi/2} \cos y~dy \right) \\ &=& \bigg( -\cos x\bigg|_{x=0}^{x=\pi/2} \bigg) \bigg( \sin y \bigg|_{y=0}^{y=\pi/2} \bigg) \\ &=& 1. \end{eqnarray*}


Double Integrals Over General Regions

In this section, we have two main goals: first, to define the double integral of a function $f(x, y)$ over regions $D$ that are more general than rectangles; second, to develop a method for evaluating this type of integral. To achieve this, we will introduce two specific types of subsets in the $xy$-plane and then extend the concept of the double integral to these regions.

To define a double integral over a non-rectangular region $D$, we consider a rectangular $R$ which encloses $D$ and define

\[ F(x,y) = \left\{ \begin{array}{rl} f(x,y) & \text{if }(x,y)\in D\\ 0, &\text{if }(x,y)\in R \text{ but } \notin D \end{array} \right. \]

Then we can define the double integral of $f(x,y)$ over $D$ by \[ \iint_D f(x,y) dA= \iint_R F(x,y)dA \]

  1. Chop $R$ into subrectangles,
  2. Compute $F(x^*,y^*)\Delta A$ for each rectangle contained in $R,$
  3. Add up the contributions from all subrectangles, and
  4. Take a limit as we chop $R$ into smaller and smaller pieces.

Then

\begin{eqnarray*} \iint_D f(x,y) ~dA & = & \iint_R F(x,y)~dA\\ &=& \lim_{\Delta A\to 0} \sum_{\text{subrectangles in }R}F\left(x^*,y^*\right)\Delta A \\ &=& \lim_{\Delta A\to 0} \sum_{\text{subrectangles in }D}f\left(x^*,y^*\right)\Delta A \end{eqnarray*}

However, this does not really tell us how to compute the double integral over $D.$ 😥

So we need to consider an alternative way of calculating the volume below a surface above a rectangle $R,$ which can be easily generalized to compute double integral over a general region. Here we need to introduce two types of plane regions.

A plane region $D$ is of type I if it lies between the graph of two continuous functions of $x.$ That is $$D=\left\{ (x,y) ~|~ a \leq x\leq b, g_1(x) \leq y \leq g_2(x) \right\}.$$

In practice, to evaluate $\iint_D f(x,y)dA$ where $D$ is a region of type I we have \[ \iint_D f(x,y)~dA = \int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x,y) ~dy~dx \]

Find $ \iint_D \left(4x+10y\right)dA$ where $D$ is the region between the parabola $y=x^2$ and the line $y=x+2.$

You can visualize the region and the solid in the following applet:

This is a type I region with $x^2 \leq y \leq x+2.$ We need to find $a$ and $b$ by solving \[ \left\{\begin{array}{ll} y=x^2\\ y=x+2 \end{array}\right. \] for $x$.

Here we have $x^2=x+2$ $\Ra x = -1, x = 2$ $\Ra a = -1, b = 2.$

Then $D= \left\{ (x,y) ~|~ -1\leq x\leq 2, ~x^2\leq y\leq x+2\right\}.$

\begin{eqnarray*} \iint_D \left(4x+10y\right)dA &=& \int_{x=-1}^{x=2} \left[ \int_{y=x^2}^{y=x+2} \left(4x+10y\right)dy \right]dx \\ &=& \int_{x=-1}^{x=2} \left[ \left( 4xy + 10 \frac{y^2}{2}\right)\bigg|_{y=x^2}^{y=x+2} \right] dx \\ &=& \int_{x=-1}^{x=2} \left[ 4x(x+2) + 5 (x+2)^2 -\left( 4x\left(x^2\right) + 5 \left(x^2\right)^2 \right) \right]dx \\ &=& \int_{x=-1}^{x=2} \left( -5 x^4 - 4 x^3 + 9 x^2 + 28 x + 20 \right) dx \\ &=& \left( -x^5 - x^4 + 3 x^3 + 14 x^2 + 20 x\right)\Bigg|_{x=-1}^{x=2} \\ &=& 81. \end{eqnarray*}

A plane region is of type II if it can be expressed by $$D=\left\{ (x,y) ~|~ c \leq y\leq d, h_1(y) \leq x \leq h_2(y) \right\}.$$

In practice, to evaluate $\iint_D f(x,y)dA$ where $D$ is a region of type II we have \[ \iint_D f(x,y)dA = \int_c^d \int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)} f(x,y) ~dx~dy \]

Find $ \iint_D xy ~dA$ where $D$ is the region between the line $y=x-1$ and the parabola $y^2=2x+6.$

You can visualize the region and the solid in the following applet:

This is a type II region. We know that $y=x-1$ and $y^2=2x+6.$ So $x=y+1$ and $x=\frac{1}{2}y^2-3.$ Then $\frac{1}{2}y^2-3\leq x\leq y+1.$

We need to find $c$ and $d$ by solving \[ \left\{\begin{array}{ll} x=\frac{1}{2}y^2-3\\ x=y+1 \end{array}\right. \] for $y$. Here we have $\frac{1}{2}y^2-3=y+1$ $\Ra y = -2, y = 4$ $\Ra c = -2, d = 4.$

Then $D= \left\{ (x,y) ~|~ -2\leq y\leq 4, ~\frac{1}{2}y^2-3\leq x\leq y+1\right\}.$

Thus

\begin{eqnarray*} \iint_D xy~dA &=& \int_{y=-2}^{y=4} \left[ \int_{x=\frac{1}{2}y^2-3}^{x=y+1} xy~ dx \right]dy \\ &=& \int_{y=-2}^{y=4} \left[ y~ \frac{x^2}{2} \bigg|_{x=\frac{1}{2}y^2-3}^{x=y+1} \right] dy \\ &=& \int_{y=-2}^{y=4} \left[ y~ \frac{(y+1)^2}{2} - y~ \frac{\left(\frac{1}{2}y^2-3\right)^2}{2} \right]dy \\ &=& \int_{y=-2}^{y=4} \left( -\frac{y^5}{8} + 2 y^3 + y^2 - 4 y \right) dy \\ &=& \left( -\frac{y^6}{48} + \frac{y^4}{2} + \frac{y^3}{3} - 2 y^2 \right)\Bigg|_{y=-2}^{y=4} \\ &=& 36. \end{eqnarray*}

In the following example, we see how it is sometimes necessary to change the order of integration in order to evaluate the integral.

Find $ \int_0^1 \int_x^1 \sin \left(y^2\right)dy~dx$

You can visualize the region and the solid in the following applet:

For $ \int \sin \left(y^2\right)dy,$ there is no anti-derivative in terms of elementary functions. 😥

Then we must change order of integration. That is, as a type II integral. In that case, we can easily compute the integral! 😃

\begin{eqnarray*} \int_\limits{x=0}^{x=1} \left[ \int_{y=x}^{y=1} \sin \left(y^2\right) dy \right]dx &=& \int_\limits{y=0}^{y=1} \left[ \int_{x=0}^{x=y} \sin \left(y^2\right) dx \right]dy \\ &=& \int_\limits{y=0}^{y=1} \bigg[ \sin \left(y^2\right) x \bigg|_{x=0}^{x=y} \bigg]dy \\ &=& \int_\limits{y=0}^{y=1} y\sin \left(y^2\right) dy \\ &=& \left( -\frac{1}{2} \cos \left(y^2\right) \right)\Bigg|_{y=0}^{y=1} \\ &=& \frac{1}{2} \big( 1 - \cos (1) \big). \end{eqnarray*}
